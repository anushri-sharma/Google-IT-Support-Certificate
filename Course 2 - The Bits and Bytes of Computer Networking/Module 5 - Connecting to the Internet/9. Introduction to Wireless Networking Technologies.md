🔌 Wireless trend and definition  
  – Fewer devices use physical cables; growth in **laptops, tablets, smartphones** drives adoption of wireless networking.  
  – **Wireless networking** = networking without wires; devices communicate via **radio waves**.

💡 Standards: IEEE 802.11 / Wi‑Fi  
  – The **IEEE 802.11** family defines how wireless devices communicate; this family is commonly called **Wi‑Fi**.  
  – Different 802.11 variants generally share protocol structure but may use **different frequency bands** and capabilities.

📶 Frequency bands and examples  
  – A **frequency band** is a designated section of the radio spectrum for specific communications.  
  – Example: **FM broadcast band = 88–108 MHz** (North America).  
  – Common Wi‑Fi bands are **2.4 GHz** and **5 GHz**.

🔁 Common 802.11 variants and evolution  
  – Frequently encountered standards: **802.11b, 802.11a, 802.11g, 802.11n, 802.11ac**.  
  – Listed in **chronological order** of adoption.  
  – Newer versions generally improve **throughput** and **concurrent-device capacity**.

🧩 OSI-layer mapping  
  – **802.11 protocols** operate at both the **physical layer** and the **data link layer** of the network model.

📦 802.11 frame overall structure (fields in order)  
  – **Frame control field (16 bits)**: contains multiple subfields describing how to process the frame (e.g., protocol version).  
  – **Duration field**: specifies how long the entire frame lasts so the receiver knows how long to listen.  
  – **Four address fields**: reason explained below.  
  – **Sequence control field (16 bits)**: holds a sequence number to maintain frame ordering.  
  – **Data payload**: carries upper-layer protocol data.  
  – **Frame check sequence (FCS)**: checksum used for **CRC** error detection (same concept as Ethernet).

🔗 Access points and network architecture (why four addresses exist)  
  – A **wireless access point (AP)** bridges the wireless and wired parts of a network; multiple APs can cover a large area.  
  – Wireless devices **associate** with an AP (usually based on proximity, signal strength, and interference) so that incoming traffic is routed by the correct AP.  
  – Four address fields are required to indicate which AP should process the frame in multi-hop or bridged scenarios.

📇 The four MAC address roles (each **6 bytes**)  
  – **Source address**: MAC of the original sending device.  
  – **Destination address**: intended recipient on the network.  
  – **Receiver address**: MAC of the AP that should **receive** the frame.  
  – **Transmitter address**: MAC of the device that just **transmitted** the frame.  
  – Typical cases: **destination = receiver** and **source = transmitter**; differences occur when frames are relayed between APs or when network architecture introduces extra hops.

🔢 Sequence control and ordering implications  
  – The **16‑bit sequence control** provides a **sequence number** to keep frame ordering correct across retransmissions and relays.  
  – Proper ordering is necessary when APs or intermediate devices forward frames.

🧾 Payload and integrity  
  – The **data payload** carries higher-layer protocol information (IP, TCP/UDP, etc.).  
  – The **FCS** checksum enables receivers to detect corrupted frames (CRC-based), mirroring Ethernet’s integrity check behavior.
