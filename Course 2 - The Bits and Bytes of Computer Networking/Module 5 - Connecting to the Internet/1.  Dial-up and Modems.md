
# [Dial-up and Modems](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dp51uP8I36I&t=3s)

💡 Early need and solution for long-distance computer communication  
  – As computer use expanded in the 20th century, networks were needed to share data over distance beyond local connections.  
  – In the late 1970s two Duke University graduate students created **Usenet**, using existing telephone infrastructure to exchange message “bulletin board” content between sites; this became a durable precursor to later dial-up networks.

📞 The network used: PSTN / POTS  
  – The Public Switched Telephone Network (PSTN), also called **Plain Old Telephone Service (POTS)**, was a mature, global infrastructure by the late 1970s and enabled long-distance data transfer without building new cabling.

🔌 Dial-up connection basics  
  – A **dial-up connection** uses POTS for data transfer and is named because the connection is established by dialing a phone number.  
  – Dial-up links were commonly used between institutions (e.g., colleges) to exchange message batches in early networking setups.

🔊 Modems: function and name  
  – **Modem** stands for **modulator–demodulator**; it converts computer data into audio-frequency signals that can travel over voice-oriented telephone lines and reconverts them at the far end.  
  – This process is analogous to line coding on wired networks, which translates binary data into electrical signal patterns for transmission.

🔔 Dial-up handshake noise  
  – The familiar screeching/warbling sounds heard when establishing a dial-up connection are the audible result of modems negotiating signaling parameters and synchronizing over the phone line.

📈 Data-rate evolution (typical historical figures)  
  – **Late 1950s:** around **110 bits/sec** (early teleprinter/terminal rates).  
  – **Late 1970s (Usenet era):** around **300 bits/sec** commonly used for data transfer across phone lines.  
  – **Early 1990s (household dial-up):** common speeds reached **14.4 kilobits/sec** and later increased (e.g., **28.8 kbps**, **56 kbps**) before broadband adoption.  
  – Improvements in modulation and encoding yielded higher throughput over time until broadband largely displaced dial-up for consumer Internet access.

⚖️ Technical nuance: baud vs bits per second  
  – **Baud** properly denotes the number of signal changes (symbols) per second, not strictly bits per second; the two are equal only when each symbol encodes a single bit.  
  – Many historical descriptions equated baud with bps for simple modems, but later modulation schemes carried multiple bits per symbol, increasing bps without increasing baud.

🧭 Persistence and relevance today  
  – Widespread broadband adoption made dial-up rare for typical users, but dial-up **still exists** in some rural or infrastructure-limited areas as a practical (sometimes only) option.  
  – Understanding dial-up and modems is relevant historically and for troubleshooting or working in environments where legacy telephone-based connectivity remains in use.

🔎 Conceptual takeaway about early networking design  
  – Early networking repurposed existing public infrastructure (PSTN) rather than inventing new cabling topologies, demonstrating a pragmatic engineering approach that enabled broader, lower-cost inter-site connectivity prior to modern packet networks.
