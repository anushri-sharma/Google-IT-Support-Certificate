🔥 TCP purpose and contrast  
  - TCP is used to establish connections for sending **long chains of segments**; lower-layer protocols (IP, Ethernet) send **individual packets**.  
  - Understanding TCP behavior and its control flags is essential for troubleshooting unexpected network traffic.

🛠️ **Six TCP control flags** (listed in header order; not necessarily by frequency or importance)  
  - **URG (urgent)**: value 1 marks the segment as urgent and directs the receiver to consult the **urgent pointer**; this feature is **rarely used** in practice.  
  - **ACK (acknowledgement)**: value 1 means the **acknowledgement number** field is valid and should be examined; used to confirm receipt of data.  
  - **PSH (push)**: asks the receiver to **push buffered data immediately to the application**, useful when sending small amounts that require immediate processing rather than waiting for a full buffer.  
  - **RST (reset)**: signals that a side cannot recover from missing/malformed segments and requests an immediate **connection reset** (start over).  
  - **SYN (synchronize)**: used to **initiate a connection** and directs the receiver to examine the **sequence number** field for initial sequence synchronization.  
  - **FIN (finish)**: value 1 indicates the sender has **no more data to send** and wishes to close the connection.

🤝 **Three-way handshake (connection establishment)**  
  - Step 1: **Computer A → Computer B: SYN**  
  - Step 2: **Computer B → Computer A: SYN/ACK** (acknowledges A's sequence and provides B's own SYN)  
  - Step 3: **Computer A → Computer B: ACK** (acknowledges B's SYN/ACK)  
  - This **SYN, SYN/ACK, ACK** exchange is the universal pattern for establishing a TCP connection and is called the **three-way handshake**.  
  - After completion the connection is established and both sides can exchange data.  
  - Because both sides have sent SYN/ACK pairs during the handshake, the connection operates in **full duplex**; each segment sent should be followed by a segment with the **ACK** flag to confirm receipt.

🔚 **Four-way handshake (connection termination)**  
  - Step 1: Side X → Side Y: **FIN** (X has no more data)  
  - Step 2: Side Y → Side X: **ACK** (acknowledges the FIN)  
  - Step 3: Side Y → Side X: **FIN** (Y is also ready to close)  
  - Step 4: Side X → Side Y: **ACK** (acknowledges Y's FIN)  
  - This four-step sequence cleanly closes both directions of data transfer.  
  - Edge case: a connection can remain **simplex** if only one side closes; this is uncommon in practice.

⚠️ Practical implications and notes  
  - **URG** is seldom encountered, so avoid assuming urgent-pointer behavior will be present.  
  - **PSH** is useful when low-latency delivery of small messages is required (e.g., interactive commands).  
  - **RST** indicates a serious protocol/state mismatch and usually requires re-establishing the connection.  
  - Consistently setting **ACK** for received segments is fundamental for reliable delivery and tracking what each side has received.
