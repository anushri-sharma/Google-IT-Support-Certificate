
# [Public DNS Servers](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ahpl63qGNmQ&t=2s)


Having functional DNS is an important part of a functional network.

In most cases, these name servers are all you really need for your computer to communicate 
with other devices on the Internet.

But most businesses also run their own DNS servers.

In the very least, this is needed to resolve names of internal hosts.

Anything from naming a computer NAIS-laptop to being able to refer to a printer by a name 
instead of an IP requires your own name server.

A third option is to use a DNS as a service provider, and it's getting more and more popular.

No matter what DNS service model you're using on your network, it's useful to have a way to test DNS 
functionality in case you suspect something isn't working right.

It can also be super useful to have a backup DNS option in case you experience problems with your own.

You might even be in the early stages of building out a new network, and even

if you plan to have your own name server eventually, it may not be ready for use.


🔥 **DNS is essential to network functionality**  
  – **ISP-provided recursive name server** is normally included with service and is usually sufficient for Internet name resolution.  
  – **Local DNS** is commonly run by businesses to resolve internal hostnames (for example, naming a laptop or a printer so users can refer to them by name rather than IP).

💡 **Three DNS deployment models and common uses**  
  – **ISP-provided**: default choice for general use; recommended outside troubleshooting.  
  – **Self-run (internal) DNS**: required for internal name resolution and custom network naming.  
  – **DNS-as-a-service (public/third-party)**: increasingly popular; useful as a backup, for troubleshooting, or while building a new network before an internal server is ready.  

🔎 **Public DNS servers — what they are and why use them**  
  – Public DNS servers are name servers made available for free public use and are handy for troubleshooting name-resolution issues.  
  – They are sometimes used as a primary resolver by individuals or organizations.  
  – Historical note: sysadmin folklore long favored Level 3’s servers as widely used public resolvers.

📡 **Notable public DNS examples and addressing**  
  – **Level 3 Communications** public DNS: **4.2.2.1 through 4.2.2.6** — easy-to-remember, widely used historically, but never officially advertised by Level 3.  

<img width="1280" height="472" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/2d1a5c3a-7d38-41f2-be5f-c5460248909b" />

  – **Google Public DNS**: **8.8.8.8** and **8.8.4.4** — officially documented and endorsed for free public use.  
  – Most public DNS providers announce their service globally via **anycast**, improving reachability and resiliency.

⚠️ **Security, privacy, and operational cautions**  
  – **Research providers before use**: malicious or misconfigured resolvers can return faulty DNS responses to redirect users to harmful sites.  
  – **Prefer ISP resolvers for routine use**; use public DNS primarily for troubleshooting or as a trusted backup.  
  – Ensure the resolver is run by a **reputable organization** to reduce risk of hijacking or data misuse.

🛠️ **Practical testing tip**  
  – Many public DNS servers respond to **ICMP echo (ping)**, making them convenient targets for basic connectivity testing in addition to DNS troubleshooting.
