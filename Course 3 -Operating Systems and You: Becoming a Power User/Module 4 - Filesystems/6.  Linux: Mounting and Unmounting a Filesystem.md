
# [Linux: Mounting and Unmounting a Filesystem](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XkeROub4UZY&t=2s)



🔥 Mounting purpose and constraint  
  – **Mounting** attaches a filesystem on a block device to a directory so the OS can read/write it.  
  – You **cannot cd into /dev/sdb** because block device nodes are not directories (error: **"device is not a directory"**).

📁 Create a mount point and mount the filesystem (example)  
  – Make a directory to serve as the mount point (example: **mkdir /myusb**).  
  – Mount with: **sudo mount /dev/sdb1 /myusb**.  
  – After mounting, you can **read and write** files at the mount point (e.g., /myusb).

♻️ Automatic mounting vs manual mounting  
  – Modern desktop OSes often **auto-mount** removable devices (USB) when plugged in.  
  – Manual mounting is required when auto-mount is not available or for explicit control.

🚪 Unmounting (safe removal)  
  – Unmount with **sudo umount /myusb** or **sudo umount /dev/sdb1** (either device or mount point works).  
  – Always **unmount** a removable drive before physically disconnecting to avoid filesystem corruption or errors.

🔁 Shutdown and mount persistence  
  – Manually-mounted filesystems are **unmounted automatically on shutdown**, and the mount point will no longer be mounted after reboot.  
  – To have a filesystem **mounted automatically at boot**, add an entry to **/etc/fstab**.

📄 /etc/fstab essentials for automatic mounts  
  – **/etc/fstab** entries list: **UUID** (or device), **mount point**, **filesystem type**, and **mount options** (plus dump/pass fields).  
  – Obtain device UUIDs with **sudo blkid** (or **lsblk -f**) and use the UUID in **/etc/fstab** for stable mounts across device name changes.

✅ Quick recap of related tasks covered  
  – Found the partition (e.g., **/dev/sdb1**) using partition-listing tools.  
  – Created a filesystem mount point directory.  
  – Mounted and accessed the filesystem with **sudo mount**.  
  – Unmounted safely with **sudo umount**.  
  – Learned to persist mounts at boot by editing **/etc/fstab** and using **sudo blkid** to get UUIDs.
