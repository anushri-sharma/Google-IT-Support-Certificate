
# [Password Attacks](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vrxY0HugE7M&t=13s)

🔐 **Passwords are the most common safeguard** for preventing unauthorized account access, but their effectiveness depends on strength and uniqueness.  
  – **Password reuse** (e.g., using the same "Fido" password across services) exposes multiple accounts if one is compromised.  

<img width="1280" height="452" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/b9ed6236-66a3-4bf0-809e-74985d163f82" />

🔓 **Password attacks** use automated tools ("password-crackers") to guess credentials by repeatedly trying possibilities until access is gained.  
  – Attack software can be highly effective against weak or reused passwords.  

<img width="1279" height="539" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/3d3d00b2-abf6-468e-aedc-22127bb534f3" />

🧱 **Brute‑force attack**: tests every possible character/letter combination until the correct password is found.  
  – Requires testing large numbers of combinations, so **time to crack increases with length and complexity**.  
  – Success depends on attacker resources (CPU/GPU power), the hashing algorithm, and any rate limits or account lockouts.  
  – **CAPTCHA** helps by distinguishing humans from automated clients, slowing or blocking automated brute‑force attempts.  
  – CAPTCHA has limitations: it can be bypassed (e.g., by distributed attacks, human-solving services, or other workarounds), so it is **not a complete defense** on its own.  

<img width="1279" height="634" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/6c863912-1f54-487b-a2ce-626fb83d82f5" />

📚 **Dictionary attack**: tries commonly used words and predictable passwords (examples: **"password"**, **"monkey"**, **"football"**) rather than all possible character combinations.  
  – Faster than pure brute force for weak, dictionary-based passwords.  

<img width="1277" height="587" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/263059d8-240f-4600-8fbd-96f2a5f6270e" />

🛡️ **Primary defenses against password attacks**: use strong, unique passwords and implement account protections.  
  – **Strong passwords**: avoid real dictionary words; use a mix of **uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols**; increase length.  
  – **Account protections**: CAPTCHA, rate limiting, account lockouts, and **multi‑factor authentication (MFA)** significantly reduce risk.  

🔍 **Example (cracking time and transformation)**: without protections, a typical cracker might break a common word like **"sandwich"** in about **one minute** — but that estimate **depends on attacker hardware and hashing**.  
  – Transforming the same word by inserting symbols, mixing case, and adding numbers (example: **s-&-n-D-w-h1ch**) preserves memorability while **substantially increasing cracking time**.  

<img width="1276" height="175" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/9549145c-b227-40d5-be9e-fd97249474bc" />

⚠️ **Edge cases and limitations** to consider beyond password strength:  
  – Strong passwords reduce brute‑force and dictionary success but do not stop phishing, credential stuffing from breaches, or attacks on weak server-side hashing.  
  – Comprehensive protection requires both strong credentials and server-side safeguards (proper hashing/salting, rate limits, MFA, monitoring).
