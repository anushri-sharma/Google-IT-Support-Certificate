🔐 **X.509 standard & CRL**  
  – **X.509** defines the format for digital certificates and a **certificate revocation list (CRL)**, a mechanism to publish certificates that are no longer valid.  
  – First issued in **1988**; the modern version is **Version 3**.

💡 **Clarification on algorithm fields (common confusion corrected)**  
  – The **Subject Public Key algorithm** (the subject’s key type) and the **Certificate Signature algorithm** (the issuer’s signing method) are separate; **they do not have to match**.  
  – The signature algorithm describes how the issuer signed the certificate; the subject public key algorithm describes the subject’s own public key.

🔎 **Certificate fingerprints**  
  – **Not fields in the certificate**, but **hash digests** computed over the whole certificate by clients for identification or validation.  
  – Clients display or compare fingerprints when inspecting certificates to ensure integrity or to verify a presented certificate.


🕸️ **Web of Trust — decentralized alternative to PKI**  
  – **Web of trust** relies on **individuals signing each other’s public keys** rather than a centralized CA issuing certificates.  
  – Before signing, a signer should **verify the person’s identity** through an agreed mechanism (examples: **driver’s license, passport**).  
  – Signing someone’s key is a **vouching act**: you assert that you trust the binding between that public key and the person.  
  – Signing is typically **reciprocal** in practice (both parties sign each other’s keys).  
  – **Key signing parties** are organized events where participants verify IDs and sign each other’s keys so every participant’s key is signed by others present.  
  – Trust extends transitively: when a participant later establishes trust with a new member, that **bridges separate webs of trust**, allowing the overall trust network to grow.
