
# [Asymmetric Cryptography](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2WZtYVai1sg&t=7s)

🔐 Asymmetric vs symmetric cryptography  
  – **Symmetric** uses the **same key** to encrypt and decrypt; **asymmetric (public key)** uses **different keys** for encryption and decryption.  
  – Each participant **generates a private key** and **derives a public key** from it; the **public key is shareable**, the **private key must remain secret**.  
  – Security depends on the **computational difficulty** of deriving the private key from the public key.

🔁 Key-generation, exchange, and encryption flow (example with Suzanne and Darryl)  
  – Both Suzanne and Darryl generate their own **private/public key pairs** and **exchange public keys**.  
  – When Suzanne wants to send Darryl a confidential message she **encrypts using Darryl’s public key**; only **Darryl’s private key** can decrypt it.  
  – When Darryl replies, he **encrypts with Suzanne’s public key**; Suzanne decrypts with her private key.  
  – This asymmetric flow ensures that **only the intended recipient** (holder of the corresponding private key) can read messages encrypted with their public key.

✍️ Digital signatures: creation and verification  
  – To prove origin and integrity, the sender **creates a digital signature** by combining the message with their **private key** (signing operation).  
  – The sender transmits both the **message and its signature**; the recipient verifies by checking the signature with the sender’s **public key**.  
  – Successful verification proves the message was signed with the sender’s private key and **has not been modified**; **any change**, even a single whitespace, causes validation to fail.  
  – Signatures provide **message origin authentication** and **tamper detection**.

⚖️ Security properties granted by asymmetric systems  
  – **Confidentiality**: achieved via encrypting with the recipient’s public key so only the recipient’s private key can decrypt.  
  – **Authenticity**: achieved via digital signatures that let recipients verify the claimed sender and that the message was not altered.  
  – **Non-repudiation**: because a valid signature implies the holder of the private key authored the message, the signer **cannot credibly deny** authorship.

⚠️ Implication if signatures are not used  
  – Without digital signatures, an attacker could encrypt a message with Darryl’s public key and **impersonate another sender** (e.g., claim it’s from Suzanne), because encryption alone does not prove origin.  
  – Therefore, **encryption + signature** is necessary when both confidentiality and assured origin/integrity are required.
