
# [Disabling Unnecessary Components](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MCPH69O39EA&t=10s)

🔥 **Zero-day vulnerabilities**  
  – **Definition:** flaws unknown until exploited in the wild, creating risk that cannot be eliminated by patching before discovery.  
  – **Implication:** design controls that reduce exposure even to unknown flaws rather than relying solely on known-patch cycles.

💡 **Risk reduction is the primary goal**  
  – **Approach:** restrict and control access to systems to lower probability that an unknown flaw can be discovered and exploited.  
  – **Outcome:** smaller exposure means lower chance of compromise from unknown vulnerabilities.

🔎 **Attack vector vs. attack surface**  
  – **Attack vector:** a method or mechanism an attacker or malware uses to gain access (examples: email attachments, network protocols/services, network interfaces, user input).  
  – **Attack surface:** the sum of all attack vectors in a system — all possible ways an external actor can interact with the system, regardless of known vulnerabilities.  
  – **Implication:** because not all vulnerabilities are known, treat every interaction avenue as potential risk and aim to minimize the attack surface.

🧩 **Keep attack surfaces as small as possible**  
  – **Rationale:** less complexity and fewer active components reduce the number of potential undiscovered flaws.  
  – **Operational guidance:** remove or disable anything not strictly required.

🔌 **Disable unnecessary services, protocols, and components**  
  – **Action:** turn off extra services or protocols that are not essential to operations.  
  – **Benefit:** each disabled service removes an active code path and a potential exploit vector.  
  – **Example:** disable management access on a switch if not used; disable vendor-specific APIs you do not plan to use.

🛡️ **Restrict access channels and enforce least privilege**  
  – **Principle:** only allow access when absolutely necessary and limit exposure via network controls.  
  – **Example:** prevent direct printer access from outside the local network by adjusting firewall rules rather than permitting broad access.

🧰 **Reduce duplicate or overlapping software deployments**  
  – **Strategy:** consolidate multiple solutions into a single unified solution where feasible.  
  – **Effect:** fewer products mean less code, reduced complexity, and fewer differing vulnerability surfaces to manage.

💻 **Apply hardening at every layer and platform**  
  – **Scope:** implement disabling and restriction measures not just on servers and network infrastructure, but also on desktops, laptops, and consumer OS defaults.  
  – **Reason:** many consumer OSes ship with default services not appropriate for enterprise environments; these defaults increase attack surface if left enabled.

⚖️ **Weigh low-risk features against unnecessary exposure**  
  – **Observation:** some services may appear low risk when behind strong firewall rules, but still represent avoidable exposure.  
  – **Guidance:** if a service is not needed, disable it — defense in depth favors layered risk mitigation over accepting avoidable risks.

🏗️ **Design for defense in depth and failure scenarios**  
  – **Concept:** implement multiple security layers so that if one control is bypassed or fails, lower layers still protect the component.  
  – **Operational question for admins:** if higher-layer controls are bypassed, how will this component remain secure?
