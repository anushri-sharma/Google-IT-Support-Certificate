# [Application Policies](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Uf7cgyd0yes&t=2s)

🔥 **Application software is a large attack surface**
  – **Application software** across a large fleet increases exposure to vulnerabilities and compromise risk.  
  – Policies are needed to both **define permitted applications** and **educate users** on secure usage.

🛡️ **Establish clear application policies**
  – Policies should explicitly state which applications are **permitted or prohibited** and outline expectations for secure behavior.  
  – Policies serve two functions: **access control** (what’s allowed) and **user guidance** (how to use software securely).

♻️ **Require and enforce timely software updates**
  – Policy should require use of the **latest version** or otherwise supported releases so **security patches** are applied.  
  – From IT support perspective, updates often **fix functionality issues**; from security perspective, they **close vulnerabilities**.  
  – Account for user friction: updates may require restarts and feel disruptive; policy should balance enforcement with operational impact.

❌ **Disallow risky classes of software**
  – **File‑sharing and piracy software** are high-risk and usually have no legitimate business use; they correlate strongly with malware and legal exposure.  
  – Policies should explicitly ban or tightly control such categories to reduce exposure and compliance risk.

🧩 **Standardize approved solutions for common tasks**
  – Identify common business needs and **select specific, approved implementations** (e.g., one approved collaboration app).  
  – Benefits: easier evaluation of security posture, uniform installations, reduced variability of attack surfaces.  
  – Rationale: multiple different apps that do the same job each introduce distinct potential vulnerabilities.

📋 **Define rules for binary whitelisting**
  – If using **binary whitelisting**, create a policy specifying **what types of software qualify** for whitelist inclusion.  
  – Require a **business use case or justification** to avoid many ad‑hoc personal requests (for example, games should not be whitelisted unless the organization is a game developer).  
  – Prevent proliferation of one‑off exceptions that weaken the whitelist model.

🌐 **Treat browser extensions and add‑ons as high‑risk**
  – Modern workflows often run in the browser; **extensions** can be a stealthy malware vector.  
  – Extensions that require **full access to visited sites** can modify pages or **exfiltrate user input** (risking confidential data).  
  – Policy should **classify risky extensions**, restrict or require approvals for certain permissions, and specify allowed/forbidden extension categories.

⚖️ **Require business justification and approval processes**
  – For exceptions, require documented **business justification** and an approval workflow to prevent arbitrary approvals.  
  – Use justification to limit unnecessary expansion of supported software and to maintain security posture.

🔍 **Minimize attack surface as a guiding principle**
  – Each distinct application that fulfills the same need is an additional potential vulnerability; consolidating reduces the number of vectors to monitor and patch.  
  – Evaluations of software choices should prioritize minimizing cumulative attack surface.

🎓 **Pair policies with user education and training**
  – Policies alone are insufficient; users need **training** to make informed choices that affect organizational security.  
  – Provide accessible materials (e.g., review internal training videos) to reinforce policy intent and practical secure behaviors.
