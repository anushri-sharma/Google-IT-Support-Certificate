 # [Best practices for authentication](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tUitmjFLO1Y&t=16s)

 🔥 Three A’s of security and focus of module  
  – **Authentication (authn)**: proving an identity (e.g., supplying a password for an email address).  
  – **Authorization (authz)**: what resources an authenticated identity may access (e.g., your inbox vs others’ inboxes).  
  – **Accounting**: listed as part of the triad but not expanded in this module.  

🔑 Identification vs authentication (email example)  
  – **Identification**: declaring a unique identity (example: **your email address** when logging in).  
  – **Authentication**: proving you are that identity (example: **password** associated with the email).  
  – Implication: identification alone does not grant access—authentication must validate the claim.

🧭 Authn vs authz distinction and terminology  
  – Security world abbreviations: **authn = authentication**, **authz = authorization**.  
  – Implication: systems must separate verifying identity (authn) from granting permissions (authz) to avoid privilege errors.

🔒 What makes a password weak (example: “ponies”)  
  – **Weak factors**: **short length (6 chars)**, **single character class (lowercase letters)**, and presence in **dictionary** files.  
  – Attack vectors: **brute force** and **dictionary-based attacks** easily compromise such passwords.  
  – Implication: short, common-word passwords provide small search space and high compromise risk.

🧩 What makes a password stronger and usability trade-offs  
  – Strength increases with **length** and **character-class diversity** (numbers, uppercase, punctuation); example of a 10-character mixed password is stronger.  
  – Usability cost: complex/random passwords are **harder to memorize**.  
  – Security vs usability trade-off: higher security often reduces usability; systems must balance both.

📝 Passphrase + leetspeak example and attacker considerations  
  – Example construct: start with a memorable phrase (“I like ponies”), use **letter→number substitutions**, change **s→z**, and append numbers.  
  – Immediate advantage: more memorable than a random string while appearing complex.  
  – Caveat: **number-letter substitutions and predictable transformations are well-known** to attackers and cracking tools, reducing effective strength.  
  – Implication: apparent complexity can be illusory if transformations are predictable.

🏛 Extreme security vs usefulness (risk mitigation principle)  
  – Risk mitigation reality: **cannot eliminate all risk**; aim to understand, reduce, and monitor risks.  
  – Illustrative extreme: a powered-off, network-isolated machine in a concrete bunker is highly secure but **functionally useless**.  
  – Implication: security controls must preserve necessary usability and function.

🧭 Organizational password policy recommendations and pitfalls  
  – Enforce **length requirements**, **character complexity**, and **dictionary-word checks** to increase baseline password strength.  
  – Prohibit **writing passwords down in plain text**, **reusing passwords across accounts**, and **sharing passwords**.  
  – Rationale: password reuse or sharing creates broad exposure—compromise of one account can lead to compromise of others and undermines identity accountability.

⏱ Password rotation: benefits and risks  
  – Benefit: **rotation reduces risk from undetected password compromise** by limiting exposure window.  
  – Risk of overly frequent rotation: too-short rotation periods cause **user inconvenience**, which often leads to poor behaviors (e.g., writing passwords on post‑its or phones).  
  – Example: forcing highly complex passwords to change every **three months** likely increases insecure workarounds.  
  – Implication: set rotation intervals to balance mitigation benefits against induced risky behaviors.
