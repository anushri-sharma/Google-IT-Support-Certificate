
# [Managing Services in Linux](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9U5lw91bTdk&t=5s)

🔧 Check and manage services (concept)
  
  – **Use service commands to check status and control services** (example: **service ntp status**).  
  – Exact commands vary by OS, but the concepts (status, stop, start, restart) are the same.

⏱ NTP daemon behavior and rationale
  
  – The NTP daemon runs to **synchronize the system clock** automatically.  
  – It corrects time by **slewing** at a rate of about **0.5 milliseconds per second** to avoid sudden jumps that would disrupt time-dependent services.  
  – If the clock change is greater than **128 milliseconds**, NTP will **not** auto-correct (it assumes an external/systemic change).

<img width="1274" height="474" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/c35f01df-2116-47da-b13b-65a1a0c2dc4e" />

🧪 Demonstration (manual time change)
  
  – Manually set the system time: **sudo date 2017-01-01 00:00:00** then verify with **date**.  
  – After this large manual change, NTP does not immediately fix the clock because it exceeds the **128 ms** threshold.

♻️ Forcing an immediate resync
  
  – NTP has a startup option that allows a **drastic adjustment** when the daemon starts (safe because it normally starts before time-dependent services).  
  – Stop and start sequence: **sudo service ntp stop** → check **date** → **sudo service ntp start** → check **date** (time is restored).  
  – Or use **sudo service ntp restart** (equivalent to stop then start) after setting the date to force the immediate correction.

🔐 Privileges and operational notes
  
  – **Any user** can check a service status.  
  – **Stopping, starting, or restarting** services requires administrative privileges (use **sudo**).  
  – The same commands/workflow apply to managing more complex services, not just NTP.


⚠️ Edge cases and implications
  
  – NTP’s slow slewing avoids disrupting dependent services when clock drift is small.  
  – Large manual changes require a restart (or other admin intervention) to apply an immediate correction.  
  – Restarting at boot is acceptable because services start before most time-sensitive processes run.
