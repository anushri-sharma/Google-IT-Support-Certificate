**Introduction to Disaster Recovery Planning**  
- Humans inevitably make mistakes or encounter accidents, including IT specialists. Examples include electrical surges damaging hardware or accidental deletion of critical folders.  
- **Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP)** is a documented set of procedures designed to prepare for, respond to, and recover from emergencies or disasters affecting IT operations.  
- The plan is comprehensive and addresses actions **before**, **during**, and **after** a disaster to ensure business continuity.

**Purpose and Scope of Disaster Recovery Plan**  
- The primary goal of a DRP is to **minimise disruption** to business and IT operations by:  
  - Reducing system downtime  
  - Preventing significant data loss  
- Contrary to what the name suggests, a DRP covers not only recovery after a disaster but also **preventive and detection measures**.

  
**Preventive Measures**  
- Preventive measures are proactive steps taken **before** a disaster to reduce its impact and downtime.  
- Examples include:  
  - Regular data backups  
  - Implementing redundant systems  
  - Designing critical infrastructure with **redundant power supplies**, often supported by different power sources such as battery backups.  
- The intent is to ensure that if one component fails, the system continues operating with minimal interruption.

**Detection Measures**  
- Detection measures allow timely identification and alerting when a disaster or failure occurs.  
- Early detection is crucial because some DRP steps are **time-sensitive** and must happen quickly to prevent data loss or hardware damage.  
- Example:  
  - In power outages, systems switch to battery backup, but these have limited duration, necessitating a graceful shutdown before power is completely lost.  
  - Systems with redundant power supplies often have alert functions that notify staff immediately upon power loss.

**Monitoring Environmental Conditions**  
- Besides power-related alerts, other environmental factors must be monitored to prevent disasters:  
  - Flood sensors to detect water ingress in server rooms  
  - Temperature and humidity sensors to identify overheating or poor moisture conditions that might damage equipment  
  - Smoke detectors and fire alarms for early fire detection  
- These detection tools enable prompt action to protect IT assets and maintain operations.


 
**People and Evacuation Considerations**  
- A disaster recovery plan must also consider the **human element** in business continuity.  
- Although building evacuation procedures primarily fall under building management, IT specialists should collaborate closely regarding:  
  - Power delivery  
  - Heating and cooling systems  
  - Building evacuation logistics  
- IT must prepare to maintain productivity during evacuations by setting up **temporary work arrangements**, such as remote working setups, ensuring employees can continue functioning effectively outside the office environment.

 
**Corrective or Recovery Measures**  
- These measures are enacted **after** the disaster has occurred and typically include:  
  - Restoring lost data from backups  
  - Rebuilding and re-configuring damaged systems  
- The recovery phase begins once the disaster has been detected and immediate mitigation steps are in place.  
- Often, disasters impact one system in a redundant pair or replication scheme, preventing total service outages initially.

**Single Point of Failure (SPOF)**  
- When one system in a redundant pair fails, it becomes a **single point of failure**, meaning the system is vulnerable to complete failure upon a subsequent fault.  
- This situation is undesirable and signals that the organisation is not fully prepared for further disasters.  
- Continuous restoration and re-establishment of redundancy are vital to avoid SPOFs and maintain resilience.

 
**Summary of Disaster Recovery Plan Elements**  
- A well-structured disaster recovery plan involves:  
  - **Preventive measures:** proactive steps to reduce disaster impact  
  - **Detection measures:** timely alerts to enable quick response  
  - **Corrective/recovery measures:** restoring normal operations after a disaster  
- Collaboration between IT support, building management, and other stakeholders is essential for effective disaster management.  
- The ultimate objective is to maintain business continuity and reduce downtime and data loss as much as possible.

**Key Insights:**  
- Disaster recovery planning is not only reactive but also involves proactive and detection strategies.  
- Redundancy and monitoring systems play a critical role in minimising downtime.  
- Human factors, such as evacuation and remote work readiness, are integral to a successful DRP.  
- Avoiding or promptly addressing single points of failure is essential for system resilience.
