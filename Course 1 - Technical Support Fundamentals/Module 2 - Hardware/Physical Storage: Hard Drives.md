

🔥 **Data size fundamentals: bit → byte → kilobyte**
  – **Bit**: smallest unit, stores a single binary value (**0** or **1**).  
  – **Byte**: **8 bits**; a single byte can represent a letter, number, or symbol.  
  – **Kilobyte (KB)**: traditionally **1,024 bytes** (binary convention).  
  – Note: storage labeling can use decimal (1 KB = 1,000 bytes) or binary (1 KB = 1,024 bytes), which affects capacity calculations.

💡 **Practical capacity example — music files**
  – Typical music file ≈ **3 MB**.  
  – A **500 GB** drive holds roughly **166,667** such files using decimal GB (500,000 MB / 3).  
  – Using binary GiB (500 × 1,024 MB = 512,000 MB) the same drive holds ≈ **170,667** files.  
  – Implication: small differences in unit convention change capacity estimates; expect approximate values.

🛡️ **Backup necessity**
  – Hard drives can fail and data loss is common; copy or save data to another location to avoid total loss.  
  – Backups should be routine to protect programs, music, pictures, and documents.

🔩 **Hard Disk Drive (HDD) — mechanical storage**
  – Storage uses spinning **platter(s)** and a mechanical read/write arm.  
  – Speed is measured in **RPM (revolutions per minute)**; higher **RPM** → faster read/write performance (example label: **5,400 RPM**).  
  – HDDs are **more affordable per gigabyte** but **prone to physical damage** because of moving parts.  
  – Typical use: high-capacity, cost-sensitive bulk storage.

⚡ **Solid State Drive (SSD) — flash storage**
  – No moving parts; stores data on **microchips** (similar principle to USB flash drives).  
  – Data access and transfer are **much faster** than HDDs; drives have slimmer form factors.  
  – SSDs are **less susceptible to mechanical failure** but are **more expensive per gigabyte**, so total capacity purchased may be lower.  
  – Typical use: system drives, applications, where performance and reliability are prioritized.

🔀 **Hybrid drives**
  – Combine **SSD-like cache** with an HDD bulk store to provide faster performance for frequently used data (e.g., OS) while keeping large-capacity HDD storage for less-critical files.  
  – Tradeoff: improved perceived performance without full SSD cost.

🔌 **Drive interfaces — how drives connect**
  – **ATA family** is common; the dominant member for consumer drives is **SATA (Serial ATA)**.  
  – **SATA** uses a single cable for data, is more efficient than older ATA variants, and is **hot-swappable** (can be plugged/unplugged without powering down systems that support it).  
  – SATA has been the de facto interface for HDDs and many SSDs.

🚀 **NVMe for high-performance SSDs**
  – As SSD speeds exceeded SATA bandwidth, **NVMe (Non-Volatile Memory Express)** was introduced to eliminate the SATA bottleneck.  
  – NVMe uses the **PCIe** bus (often via **M.2** or U.2 form factors) as a direct expansion/slot connection rather than a SATA cable, enabling **much greater throughput and lower latency**.  
  – Implication: NVMe SSDs deliver the best performance for demanding workloads; interface choice affects real-world speed.
