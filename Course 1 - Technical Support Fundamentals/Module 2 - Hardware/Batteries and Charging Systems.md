🔋 Mobile power delivery and charging methods  
  – **Rechargeable devices** carry power via batteries and can use **external chargers** for removable cells, **cradle stands**, or **wireless inductive chargers** (example: top-up by laying a phone on an inductive pad).  
  – Some devices use a **portable power supply** that both powers the device and charges the battery simultaneously.  

⚡ Charging power sources and the charging circuit  
  – **External power sources** can be a **wall outlet**, **another battery**, or **a solar panel**.  
  – A **charging circuit** manages power transfer from the external source to the battery, functioning like a small **power supply unit (PSU)**: it converts input power to the correct output for safe charging.  

🔌 Chargers, connectors, and compatibility risks  
  – Many chargers use **USB connectors**, but a wide variety of connector types exist.  
  – **Mismatching charger and device specifications** can damage the battery, the device, and the charger; verify device-charger compatibility before use.  

♻️ Battery lifespan and **charge cycles**  
  – A **charge cycle** is **one full charge and discharge** of a battery.  
  – Rechargeable batteries have a **limited lifespan measured in cycle count**; as batteries age they may **take longer to charge** and **hold less charge**.  
  – Comparing a device’s **current cycle count** to expected rates for that battery type helps estimate **remaining usable life**.  

🌡 Environmental limits and safety hazards  
  – Do not charge or discharge batteries outside their **safe operating temperature range**; **very cold or very hot** conditions can damage batteries.  
  – Damaged batteries can **swell, rupture, and catch fire**; handling a damaged battery requires knowing safe procedures before attempting any work.  

🛠 Battery replacement and device design implications  
  – Some devices have **user-replaceable batteries**; others (small laptops, many mobile devices) have batteries that are **difficult to replace**.  
  – When a battery reaches end-of-life it must be **replaced**; device design affects whether replacement is simple or requires trained service.  

📉 Device behavior related to aging batteries  
  – Devices may **slow performance** intentionally when a battery is aging to extend usable life.  
  – **Unexpected shutdowns** or much slower behavior can indicate **battery degradation** and should prompt a battery check.  

👩‍💻 IT support roles and troubleshooting basics  
  – IT support may be trained to **replace batteries** or coordinate sending devices out for battery replacement and returning them to end-users.  
  – First troubleshooting step: confirm the **charger, battery, and device are designed to work together**.  
  – Further troubleshooting and repair logistics are covered in additional material; for mobile platforms, familiarize yourself with **iOS and Android battery-preserving practices** to advise end-users.
