🔥 **Motherboard — core functions**
  
  – **Routes power** from the **power supply (PSU)** to components.  
  – **Provides communication fabric** connecting CPU, RAM, storage, GPU, and peripherals.  
  – **Hosts expansion cards** to expand functionality (e.g., add or upgrade a GPU, network card, etc.).  

🧩 **Chipset — how components talk**
  
  – **Chipset** is the motherboard controller that manages data flows between **CPU**, **RAM**, and peripherals.  
  – Historically split into **Northbridge** and **Southbridge**:  
  –  – **Northbridge** handled high-speed links (CPU ↔ RAM, CPU ↔ graphics).  
  –  – **Southbridge** handled I/O controllers (storage, USB, other input/output devices).  
  –  – **Modern CPUs often integrate Northbridge functions** into the CPU die; a separate Northbridge chip may not be present.  

⇆ **Expansion slots and PCI Express**
  
  – **Expansion slots** let you add capabilities by inserting expansion cards (small circuit boards) into slots on the motherboard.  
  – The current standard is **PCI Express (PCIe)**; a **PCIe slot** on the board accepts **PCIe expansion cards** (common for GPUs, NVMe adapters, etc.).  
  – Practical implication: upgrading hardware requires matching the card to an available compatible slot and physical space on the board.  

📐 **Form factor — physical size and capacity**
  
  – **Form factor** = motherboard physical dimensions and mounting/connector layout; it determines how much hardware the board can accommodate.  
  – **ATX (Advanced Technology eXtended)** is the most common desktop form factor; **full-size ATX** offers the most slots and expansion space.  
  – **ITX (Information Technology eXtended)** family provides much smaller boards; common variants include **mini-ITX, nano-ITX, pico-ITX**.  
  – **Small-system examples**: compact systems (e.g., small NUC-style PCs) use smaller form factors derived from ITX families.  
  – Trade-offs: **smaller form factor = less expansion and fewer slots** but smaller physical footprint; **larger form factor = more expansion, more cooling/drive bays, larger chassis**.  
  – **Form factor influences which expansion slots and how many devices** you can add and the overall system capability.  

🔎 **Compatibility and troubleshooting implications**
  
  – **Component compatibility depends on the motherboard**: **RAM module type**, **CPU socket**, and available expansion interfaces must match the board.  
  – Before replacing or upgrading parts, verify **physical fit** (slot type, card length/height) and **electrical/connector compatibility** (PCIe version, CPU socket, RAM standard).  
  – Example: buying a replacement graphics card that physically or electrically does not fit the motherboard results in a failed upgrade and unnecessary work.  

💡 **Peripherals and I/O**
  
  – **Peripherals** are external devices connected to the motherboard (mouse, keyboard, monitor, USB devices).  
  – **Southbridge/I/O controllers** (or equivalent integrated controllers) manage these input/output connections (storage, USB ports, etc.), so motherboard choice affects available I/O options.
